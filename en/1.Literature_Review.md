# Literature Review

First, it is important to clarify the purpose of a literature review. Generally, a literature review aims to establish a foundational understanding of a research field, including the research questions, methods, hotspots, and trends within the target area. This process helps you determine your own research direction and lays the groundwork for paper reading and subsequent work. If a master's or PhD program aims to make you an expert in the field, mastering and practicing literature review can make you "look like an expert" in the field.

The literature review process can be roughly divided into three parts: finding papers, organizing papers, and tracking academic progress.

## Finding Papers

In the first stage of finding papers, most people do not have a specific research direction and are exploring within the lab's research area. Therefore, it is necessary to quickly build a basic understanding of the field. At this point, it is recommended to prioritize searching for review papers and some excellent doctoral dissertations, as these are information-dense and often subdivide the field further. Keywords for reviews include "survey" and "review".

While reading, you should not only learn about outstanding achievements in the field but also sense the development trends and the pros and cons of various research methods. Organizing these insights into notes is a good practice. It is recommended to focus on recording key papers in the field and to note down certain keywords for future literature searches.

The second stage involves a deeper analysis of one or several subfields, using multiple keywords to search for papers and collecting high-relevance, high-quality papers for in-depth reading.

Note: If there are senior students in your lab working in the same field, you can ask them to recommend relevant reviews and key papers. Some mature labs may even have their own literature databases. Communicating with them is also an important part of academic life.

## Organizing Papers

The three parts of literature review are deeply intertwined. Organizing papers means saving highly relevant papers or reviews to your own literature database. Information to save includes (but is not limited to): paper title, year, PDF link, open-source code link, etc. When building your own database, it is best to summarize each paper in one sentence for quick recall later. ## Tracking Academic Progress

"Learning alone without friends leads to ignorance and narrow-mindedness." In academic life, we also need to track the latest developments. Common methods include:

- Twitter (X): Many researchers share their latest work on social media. By following and interacting with key figures, you will see more relevant content.

- Arxiv: Many people upload preprints before submitting to journals/conferences. As an open-access digital system, it covers many disciplines, with a high frequency of AI-related papers. Recommended [MyArxiv](https://github.com/MLNLP-World/MyArxiv) project for daily updates, and [zotero-arxiv-daily](https://github.com/TideDra/zotero-arxiv-daily) for AI-powered recommendations.

- Academic conference/journal announcements: Regularly check for the latest conference/journal results, especially for top venues in your field, and read the latest work.

- [GitHub](https://github.com/): Follow academic labs or key researchers to see their starred or open-sourced repositories. Searching with the "awesome" keyword can also find curated paper collections, e.g.:

Explanation of computer science conference/journal rankings:

In computer science, the CCF recommended list and CAS divisions are often mentioned. There are also "top conferences/journals" in each field, e.g., the four top security conferences: CCS, NDSS, S&P, and USENIX Security.

- CCF: Issued by the China Computer Federation, classifies journals and conferences into A, B, and C levels.

- JCR: Issued by Clarivate, ranks journals into four categories based on citation data.

- CAS: Issued by the Chinese Academy of Sciences, also divides into four categories, though some lists are controversial.

Note: SCI (Science Citation Index) is a literature database created by ISI. "Divisions" refer to the ranking of SCI journals by impact factor (IF) to evaluate academic influence more precisely.

Recommended tools:

- [call4paper](https://www.call4papers.cn/ccf/ccf-1.html): Search for CCF rankings and deadlines (also has a WeChat account and mini-program).
